




## **1. Introduction: What SMRs Are and Why They Matter**

**Definition:**

- SMRs are nuclear fission reactors with electrical output typically ≤300 MWe.
    
    - **Megawatt electric (MWe):** measure of electrical power output. 1 MWe = 1 million watts of electricity.
        
- Designed to be **modular**: units are built in factories and shipped to sites for assembly.
    

**Why size matters:**

- Smaller cores produce less heat per unit volume, which makes **passive cooling** feasible.
    
- Passive cooling = heat removal without pumps or active machinery—relies on physics (convection, gravity).
    

**Advantages:**

- **Modularity & Factory Fabrication:** Each module is pre-tested → reduces on-site construction errors.
    
- **Lower upfront capital costs:** Small modules are easier to finance individually.
    
- **Enhanced Safety:** Smaller cores → lower decay heat → easier to remove heat during shutdown.
    
- **Flexible Deployment:** Can fit small grids, remote sites, or industrial processes like hydrogen or district heating.
    

**Challenges:**

- **FOAK risk:** First-of-a-kind reactors are expensive and face regulatory uncertainty.
    
- **Fuel supply:** Many advanced SMRs require **HALEU** (High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium, 5–20% U‑235) which is not yet widely available.
    
- **Regulatory complexity:** Existing regulations are tailored to large reactors; smaller designs require adaptation.

**Explanations of Key Terms:**

- **Coolant:** Fluid that removes heat from the core (water, helium, liquid metal, molten salt).
    
- **Moderator:** Material that slows neutrons (water, graphite) to increase fission probability.
    
- **Fuel:** Material that undergoes fission (UO₂, MOX, TRISO particles).
    
- **Thermal vs Fast Spectrum:** Thermal = slow neutrons (~0.025 eV); Fast = high-energy (~1 MeV). Thermal → easier fission for U-235; Fast → enables breeding.
    
- **Breeding:** Conversion of fertile material (e.g., U-238 → Pu-239) to create new fissile fuel.

## **3. Macro Engineering Decisions**

### **Site Selection**

- **Geology & Seismic Risk:** Reactor foundation must resist earthquakes; important for passive safety.
    
- **Water vs Air Cooling:** Water more efficient; air is simpler but requires larger heat exchangers.
    
- **Grid Connection:** Small SMRs handle low-load grids better, avoiding voltage instability.
    

### **Modularity Impacts**

- Modules are limited in size by road/rail/sea transport dimensions → affects factory design, on-site assembly, crane requirements.
    

### **Balance of Plant (BOP)**

- Secondary systems (turbines, generators, cooling towers) convert heat → electricity or process heat.
    
- **Integral designs**: everything in one vessel → fewer connections, less piping, smaller footprint.
    
- **Split-loop designs**: separate components → easier maintenance but larger footprint.
    

### **Grid Integration**

- SMRs must handle **minimum stable load**: the smallest power output without reactor instability.
    
- May include **batteries or other storage** to smooth output.
    

### **Constructability**

- Shorter schedules reduce financing costs. Logistics of oversized modules can limit deployment if not planned.

## **4. Fuel Choices & Fuel Cycle**

**Fuel Forms Explained:**

- **UO₂ (LEU)**: Uranium dioxide pellets; standard for LWRs.
    
- **HALEU (High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium)**: 5–20% U‑235; allows long-life, compact cores.
    
- **TRISO Particles**: Uranium kernel coated with pyrolytic carbon and silicon carbide → traps fission products.
    
- **Metallic fuels (U-Zr, U-Mo, MOX)**: High thermal conductivity → lower fuel temperature gradients.
    
- **Molten salt fuels**: Dissolved uranium/thorium; allows continuous removal of fission products.
    

**Fuel Cycle Terms:**

- **Burnup:** Energy extracted per mass of fuel (MWd/kg). Higher burnup → longer life.
    
- **Fertile vs Fissile:** Fissile (U-235, Pu-239) undergo fission directly. Fertile (U-238, Th-232) → converted to fissile by neutron absorption.
    
- **Proliferation risk:** Higher enrichment (HALEU) increases risk of diversion for weapons → requires safeguards.
    

---

## **5. Materials & Mechanical Design**

**Primary Materials:**

- **Cladding:** Tube around fuel; prevents fission product release. Zirconium alloys used in LWRs (low neutron absorption, good strength, water-compatible).
    
- **Structural materials:** Stainless steel, Ni-based alloys → resist corrosion, radiation damage.
    
- **Composites:** SiC for high temp/accident tolerance.
    

**Radiation Effects:**

- Neutron irradiation causes embrittlement, swelling, helium production inside metals.
    

**Corrosion Considerations:**

- Water → stress corrosion cracking.
    
- Sodium → reacts violently with water/air.
    
- Molten salts → fluoride corrosion, requires compatible alloys.
    

---

## **6. Neutronics & Core Physics**

**Why It Matters:**

- Core must sustain controlled chain reaction (criticality).
    
- Determines fuel efficiency, safety, and core lifetime.
    

**Key Terms:**

- **Neutron Flux (φ):** Number of neutrons per area per second; impacts reaction rate.
    
- **k-effective (k_eff):** Ratio of neutrons in successive generations; k=1 → critical, k<1 → subcritical, k>1 → supercritical.
    
- **Reactivity (ρ):** Measure of departure from criticality.
    

**Reactivity Control:**

- Control rods, burnable poisons (materials absorbing neutrons gradually), soluble absorbers (boron in LWR coolant).
    

**Transient Terms:**

- **Delayed neutrons:** Neutrons emitted by some fission products; essential for safe reactor control.
    
- **Prompt neutrons:** Emitted immediately; too fast to control alone.
    

---

## **7. Thermal-Hydraulics & Heat Removal**

**Principles:**

- **Natural Circulation:** Heat causes density difference → coolant flows passively.
    
- **Two-phase flow:** Steam/water mixture; critical to avoid “dryout” (fuel overheating).
    
- **Decay Heat:** Heat produced after reactor shutdown from radioactive decay; ~7% of full power initially, decreases over time.
    

**Why It Matters:** Ensures core does not overheat even if pumps fail.

## **8. Safety Systems & Accident Behaviour**

**Defense in Depth:** Multiple layers:

1. **Fuel cladding:** First barrier to fission product release.
    
2. **Primary vessel:** Pressure boundary.
    
3. **Containment structure:** Prevents environmental release.
    
4. **Passive systems:** Rely on physics, not active controls.
    
5. **Procedures / training:** Human layer to detect, mitigate issues.
    

**Accident Terms:**

- **LOCA:** Loss of coolant accident.
    
- **Station Blackout:** Loss of offsite and onsite power.
    
- **PRA (Probabilistic Risk Assessment):** Quantifies likelihood of different accident scenarios.

- **Combined license:** Design + site approval.

# **15. Reactor Components & Operation: How SMRs Work**

This section explains **the physical parts of SMRs, their function, and how nuclear reactions are controlled**, with detailed explanations for each reactor type.

---

## **15.1 Reactor Components – General Overview**

All nuclear reactors, including SMRs, share a common set of **core components**:

| Component                           | Function                                        | Notes/Why it Matters                                                       |
| ----------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Fuel**                            | Contains fissile material (U-235, Pu-239, etc.) | Fission releases energy as heat; fuel form determines burnup and core life |
| **Cladding**                        | Encases fuel                                    | Prevents radioactive fission products from escaping into coolant           |
| **Moderator**                       | Slows neutrons                                  | Slower neutrons increase probability of fission in thermal reactors        |
| **Control rods / absorbers**        | Adjust reactivity                               | Neutron absorbers that can stop or slow the chain reaction                 |
| **Coolant**                         | Removes heat from core                          | Transfers heat to turbines or heat exchangers; critical for safety         |
| **Pressure vessel / core vessel**   | Structural containment of core                  | Maintains mechanical integrity, holds coolant under pressure               |
| **Reflectors / radial shields**     | Redirect neutrons                               | Improve neutron economy, reduce leakage, protect structures                |
| **Containment / safety structures** | Prevent release of radiation                    | Final barrier to protect public and environment                            |

---

## **15.2 How a Nuclear Chain Reaction Works (Bottom-Up Explanation)**

1. **Fission basics:**
    
    - A heavy nucleus (e.g., U-235) absorbs a neutron → becomes unstable → splits into two smaller nuclei + energy (~200 MeV) + 2–3 neutrons.
        
    - These released neutrons can **cause more fissions** → chain reaction.
        
2. **Criticality:**
    
    - **Subcritical (k<1):** Each generation produces fewer neutrons → reaction dies out.
        
    - **Critical (k=1):** Neutrons produced = neutrons lost → steady power.
        
    - **Supercritical (k>1):** Reaction grows → power increases.
        
3. **Reactivity control:**
    
    - **Control rods:** Absorb neutrons, reducing k.
        
    - **Burnable poisons:** Absorb excess neutrons over time, flattening power distribution.
        
    - **Moderator:** Slows neutrons → higher fission probability in thermal reactors.
        
4. **Start-up & shutdown:**
    
    - Reactor is started by gradually removing control rods or reducing soluble absorbers.
        
    - Reactor is stopped by fully inserting control rods or adding neutron absorbers (scram).

## **15.3 Reactor-Specific Operations & Parts**

### **A. Integral PWR (e.g., NuScale)**

**Parts & Build:**

- Reactor vessel contains core, integral steam generators, pressurizer.
    
- Coolant: pressurized water circulates through fuel → steam generators → turbines.
    
- Control rods inserted from the top; soluble boron in water fine-tunes reactivity.
    

**How Reactions Are Started & Sustained:**

1. Control rods partially withdrawn → neutrons initiate chain reaction.
    
2. Soluble boron concentration adjusted → achieves criticality.
    
3. Heat removed by pressurized water, converted to steam, drives turbines.
    

**How Reactions Are Stopped:**

- Fully insert control rods → absorb neutrons, reaction stops immediately.
    
- Residual decay heat removed via passive cooling systems.
    

**Neutronics Notes:**

- **Thermal spectrum:** Water slows neutrons.
    
- **Delayed neutrons:** Essential for controlling reactor safely.
    
- **k_eff controlled** by rod position and boron concentration.
    

---

### **B. BWRX-300 (Boiling Water Reactor SMR)**

**Parts & Build:**

- Core inside pressure vessel; water boils directly in core → steam goes to turbine.
    
- Control rods enter from bottom; no soluble boron in design (simplified).
    

**Starting the Reactor:**

1. Reactivity increased by withdrawing control rods.
    
2. Boiling stabilizes neutron moderation → self-regulating power feedback.
    

**Stopping the Reactor:**

- Scram (rapid rod insertion) → absorbs neutrons → stops fission.
    
- Steam condenses → heat removed from core.
    

**Neutronics Simplified:**

- Boiling reduces water density → less moderation → negative feedback → power self-limits.
    

---

### **C. HTGR (High-Temperature Gas Reactor)**

**Parts & Build:**

- Fuel: TRISO particles embedded in graphite pebbles or blocks.
    
- Coolant: Helium gas circulates through core.
    
- Moderator: Graphite slows neutrons.
    
- Reactor vessel surrounded by reflector, heat exchangers extract thermal energy.
    

**Starting Reactions:**

- Helium circulates, control rods adjust neutrons.
    
- Pebbles/blocks allow long burnup → slow approach to criticality.
    

**Stopping Reactions:**

- Insert control rods or reduce flow → reaction dies.
    
- Passive cooling through graphite heat capacity prevents overheating.
    

**Neutronics Notes (Bottom-Up):**

- Graphite moderator slows neutrons → increases fission probability in U-235/Th-232.
    
- TRISO coatings trap fission products → extremely robust to accidents.
    

---

### **D. Molten Salt Reactor (MSR)**

**Parts & Build:**

- Core: molten fluoride/chloride salt contains fissile fuel.
    
- Heat exchangers remove energy.
    
- Pump circulates fuel, but some designs allow natural circulation.
    

**Starting Reactions:**

- Fissile fuel in salt → heat rises, starts natural circulation.
    
- Control rods or neutron absorbers fine-tune reactivity.
    

**Sustaining Reaction:**

- Molten fuel allows online fission product removal → stable neutron economy.
    
- Some designs include fertile material → breeding new fuel.
    

**Stopping Reactions:**

- Freeze plug melts → salt drains into subcritical storage tank → chain reaction stops.
    
- Decay heat removed passively through tank walls.
    

**Neutronics Simplified:**

- Fast or thermal spectrum depending on design.
    
- Liquid fuel → continuous mixing → uniform neutron flux.
    

---

### **E. Fast Liquid-Metal Reactors (Sodium / Lead)**

**Parts & Build:**

- Core contains metallic fuel rods; coolant = sodium or lead.
    
- No moderator (fast reactor).
    
- Primary circuit transfers heat to secondary circuit (Na or Pb → water).
    

**Starting Reactions:**

- With no moderator, fast neutrons drive fission in U-238/Pu-239.
    
- Control rods adjust reactivity.
    

**Sustaining Reaction:**

- Fast neutrons maintain chain reaction; coolant transfers heat efficiently.
    

**Stopping Reactions:**

- Insert control rods → absorb fast neutrons → stop fission.
    
- Sodium reacts with air/water → special safety measures (inert cover gas, leak detection).
    

**Neutronics Notes:**

- Fast spectrum → higher breeding potential (U-238 → Pu-239).
    
- Requires careful material selection to handle neutron damage.
    

---

### **F. Microreactors**

**Parts & Build:**

- Ultra-compact cores, often TRISO or metallic fuel.
    
- Passive cooling via conduction/convection, sometimes small Na or He loops.
    

**Operations:**

- Start: rods removed or small external neutron source initiates reaction.
    
- Sustain: passive cooling + carefully balanced reactivity.
    
- Stop: rods inserted or natural negative feedback from fuel temperature halts reaction.
    

**Neutronics Notes:**

- High safety margin due to small size.
    
- Heat-to-power ratio manageable → passive shutdown feasible.
    

---

## **16. Key Takeaways from Reactor Operations**

1. **All reactors rely on neutron chain reactions**, but the spectrum, moderator, and fuel type determine how easily the reaction is started, controlled, and stopped.
    
2. **Control systems vary by design**, from control rods + soluble absorbers (LWRs) to freeze plugs (MSRs) or negative temperature feedback (HTGRs).
    
3. **Passive safety is easier in SMRs** due to smaller core size, high surface-area-to-volume ratio, and modular design.
    
4. **Materials & coolant choice impact every aspect**: heat transfer, neutron moderation, chemical safety, and long-term operation.

## **17. Reliability Comparison**

---

## **17.1. Fast Liquid-Metal (FLM) Reactors – Sodium/Lead**

**Why you see danger:**

- **Reactive coolant:** Sodium reacts violently with water and air → even small leaks can cause fire/explosion.
    
- **High heat density:** Fast cores have very little margin for decay heat removal if coolant circulation fails.
    
- **Corrosion & material stress:** Liquid metal embrittles certain steels; leaks are hard to detect before damage.
    

**Mitigation:**

- Inert cover gases (argon) and secondary loops.
    
- Strict material and leak detection protocols.
    

**Bottom line:** Risk is mostly chemical + fast-reactor dynamics. Safety is feasible but operationally challenging.

---

## **17.2. Boiling Water Reactors (BWRs)**

**Why you see danger:**

- **Two-phase flow:** Boiling inside the core → steam bubbles reduce water density locally → creates **non-linear, hard-to-predict power distributions**.
    
- **Void coefficient issues:** If water boils too much, neutron moderation drops → negative feedback, but in certain transients, power spikes can occur.
    
- **Water chemistry:** Impurities can accelerate corrosion.
    

**Mitigation:**

- Careful thermal-hydraulic design and monitoring.
    
- Control rods + automated scram systems.
    

**Bottom line:** Steam generation in-core adds complexity; small mismanagement or unanticipated boiling patterns can stress fuel cladding.

---

## **17.3. High-Temperature Gas Reactors (HTGRs)**

**Why you see danger:**

- **High-temperature helium:** Loss of containment → rapid heat escape.
    
- **Low density:** Helium has low heat capacity → leaks reduce cooling efficiency.
    
- **Graphite moderator:** If breached, can oxidize in air → safety hazard.
    

**Mitigation:**

- Use of robust TRISO fuel.
    
- Leak-tight vessel design.
    
- Passive decay heat removal.
    

**Bottom line:** HTGRs are inherently safe under normal operation, but **mechanical or seal failures** at high temperatures are critical to manage.

---

## **17.4. Pressurized Water Reactors (PWRs)**

**Why relatively safe:**

- **Stable coolant:** Water under pressure → predictable flow and boiling suppressed.
    
- **Mature technology:** Hundreds of LWRs operating → proven safety and control systems.
    
- **Passive feedback:** Negative temperature coefficient keeps power self-limiting.
    

**Limitation:**

- Pressure vessel must withstand ~150 atm; leaks can be catastrophic if not managed.
    
- Limited to standard fuel forms (UO₂, LEU).
    

---

## **17.5. Molten Salt Reactors (MSRs)**

**Why potentially safer than PWRs:**

- **Predictable fluid dynamics:** Molten salt has high heat capacity, low vapor pressure → no risk of boiling inside core.
    
- **Passive shutdown options:** Freeze plug drains fuel into subcritical tank.
    
- **High operating temperatures:** Enables higher thermal efficiency (more electricity per unit of heat).
    
- **Accident tolerance:** Liquid fuel allows redistribution of fission products; TRISO-like particles can also be embedded for safety.
    

**Considerations:**

- Corrosion of materials by hot salt → engineering challenge, but predictable.
    
- Chemical handling is required for online reprocessing if desired.
    
- Fast/thermal spectrum can be selected depending on design → flexibility.
